# Specialist Gap: Trauma-Informed Design

## Problem

The social psychologist has been providing guidance on user safety, alert language, and health feature risks. However, several questions are beyond social psychology's scope:

- How do trauma survivors (freeze, fawn, dissociation responses) interact with devices under duress?
- What do prosecutors and victim advocates actually need as evidence?
- How do abusers specifically discover and weaponize technology in DV contexts?
- Safety planning methodology — this is a professional discipline with established practices
- The difference between "helpful wellness feature" and "liability that gets someone hurt"

## What We Need

A **trauma-informed design specialist** — someone with:
- Clinical psychology background specializing in DV/IPV/stalking trauma
- Understanding of trauma responses and how they affect technology interaction
- Forensic evidence experience (what courts actually accept)
- Knowledge of abuser tactics around technology (coercive control, surveillance, weaponization of data)
- Familiarity with safety planning as practiced by DV advocates

## Closest Real-World Resources

- **NNEDV Safety Net** (techsafety.org) — the leading organization for technology safety in DV contexts
- **Clinic to End Tech Abuse** (Cornell Tech) — academic research on tech-enabled abuse
- **National Network to End Domestic Violence** — policy and practice guidance
- **WESNET** (Australia) — technology safety research

## Questions for This Specialist

1. Is a "wellness device with hidden surveillance detection" a good idea, or does it create more risk than it mitigates?
2. What is the right approach to alerts when the device is in disguise mode and the user may be in the physical presence of their abuser?
3. Should the device have a quick-wipe at all, or does that enable evidence destruction?
4. What evidence preservation practices do prosecutors and advocates actually recommend?
5. How should the device handle the scenario where the abuser finds it and demands to see what it does?
6. Is the pedometer-as-cover-story robust enough, or will a technically sophisticated abuser see through it?

## Impact on Development

Until this specialist weighs in, we should be conservative:
- Handheld stays as pedometer + hidden surveillance detection
- Sensitive health features stay on the phone app only
- No quick-wipe — if data needs protection, it belongs on the phone
- Alert-in-disguise mechanism should be subtle and reviewed before implementation
- No features that could be interpreted as "entrapment" or "spying back"
